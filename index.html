From Understanding to Control

I am a theoretical physicist and Ph.D. candidate at Rice University. My research bridges the gap between foundational condensed matter theory and the frontiers of quantum control.

My work begins with uncovering the universal principles of quantum criticality and strange metallicity, and pivots toward actively engineering these states using cavity QED, Floquet dynamics, and quantum metrology.

Research Highlights

Probing Strange Metallicity with Shot Noise

Science (2023) PRR (2024)

Strange metals defy the quasiparticle paradigm of standard Fermi liquids. In a collaborative study, we observed a surprising suppression of the Fano factor in a heavy fermion strange metal. I developed the theoretical framework demonstrating that this suppression is a direct signature of quasiparticle loss, establishing shot noise as a powerful diagnostic for non-Fermi liquid physics.

Cavity-Engineered Quantum Matter

Nature Submission Active Control

Pivoting from probing to control, I investigate how coupling quantum critical systems to optical cavities can modify emergent behavior. My recent work shows that critical spin fluctuations can amplify cavity response, driving a superradiant phase transition and enhancing intrinsic squeezing. This provides a blueprint for engineering new phases of matter using light-matter interfaces.

Foundational Theory: Kondo-Destruction QCP

Field Theory Renormalization Group

To understand the competition between screening and magnetism, I developed a novel field-theoretic renormalization group framework for the Kondo lattice. This work identifies a Kondo-destruction quantum critical point where both magnetism ceases and Landau quasiparticles lose their identity, providing strong theoretical support for the global phase diagram of heavy fermion systems.
